Eigenvectors and Eigenvalues

Interactive explainer

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors help explain how step-by-step systems evolve, from repeated linear transforms to population models, migration dynamics, and oscillating spirals.

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are instrumental to understanding electrical circuits, mechanical systems, ecology, and even Google's PageRank algorithm. Visualization helps make these ideas more intuitive.

To begin, let $v$ be a vector (shown as a point) and $A$ be a matrix with columns $a_1$ and $a_2$ (shown as arrows). If we multiply $v$ by $A$, then $A$ sends $v$ to a new vector $Av$.

If you can draw a line through the three points $(0,0)$, $v$, and $Av$, then $Av$ is just $v$ multiplied by a number $\lambda$; that is, $Av = \lambda v$. In this case, $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue and $v$ is an eigenvector. For example, here $(1,2)$ is an eigenvector and $5$ is an eigenvalue.

Below, change the columns of $A$ and drag $v$ to be an eigenvector. Every point on the same line as an eigenvector is an eigenvector, so those lines are eigenspaces. If you place $v$ on an eigenspace with $\lambda < 1$, then $Av$ moves closer to $(0,0)$; when $\lambda > 1$, it moves farther away.

What are eigenvalues and eigenvectors good for?

If you keep multiplying $v$ by $A$, you get a sequence ${ v, Av, A^2v, }$ and so on. Eigenspaces attract that sequence, while eigenvalues tell you whether it collapses to $(0,0)$ or runs away. These values tell us about systems that evolve one step at a time.

Let's explore a few applications and properties of those sequences.

It always has $\lambda = 1$ as its largest eigenvalue, so there is a value of $v_t$ for which $Av_t = v_t$.
